## Snapshot
The only scaled IDM left that straddles both sides of the chip value chain: it **designs** x86 CPUs / accelerators / custom silicon (sold through the **Intel Products** business — segments **CCG** client PCs and **DCAI** data-center & AI) and **manufactures** them (plus, increasingly, chips for external customers) through **Intel Foundry** — leading-edge nodes (Intel 3, 18A, 14A) and advanced packaging (EMIB/EMIB-T, Foveros). Three reportable segments — CCG, DCAI, Intel Foundry — plus an "all other" bucket (Mobileye; Altera deconsolidated after the 51% divestiture closed 2025-09-12) (10-K FY25, 2026-01-23). FY25 revenue $52.9B, roughly flat y/y on supply constraints (Q4 FY25 call, 2026-01-22). The entire thesis is the turnaround under CEO Lip-Bu Tan (in seat ~15 months): get **18A** to yield, win a **first major external 14A/foundry customer**, and turn the foundry P&L from a ~$2.4B/qtr loss toward break-even by exit-2027 — against a near-breakeven product P&L and a heavily restructured, government- and partner-recapitalized balance sheet.

## Position in the value chain
Intel is the rare name that occupies the **middle box and reaches into both neighbors**: it buys WFE/materials from the same suppliers as TSMC, designs its own CPUs/accelerators, fabs them in-house, and now sells leading-edge wafers + advanced packaging to *external* customers — i.e. it competes with its own foundry suppliers' biggest customer (TSMC) while remaining their customer. The swing factors are the **18A ramp** (yields, Panther Lake → Nova Lake → server) and **landing the first big external foundry/14A customer** plus the **packaging-as-trojan-horse** wins (EMIB/EMIB-T).

## Current state (latest quarter)
**Q1 FY26 (call 2026-04-23; quarter ended 2026-03-28):** Revenue **$13.6B**, +7% y/y, beating the guide midpoint by **$1.4B** — sixth straight beat. **Non-GAAP GM 41%** (~650bps above guide); **non-GAAP EPS $0.29**. But GAAP printed an operating loss of **$(3.1B)** and **EPS $(0.73)** on a **$4.07B restructuring charge** — the headline that auto-injected financials will show as near-breakeven once normalized (10-Q, 2026-04-24).
- **CCG $7.7B** (-6% q/q, above plan); FY26 PC unit TAM guided down low-double-digit %, supply-constrained, sell-in holding up better than sell-through on historically low channel inventory (StoneX, 2026-05-12).
- **DCAI $5.1B** (+7% q/q, +22% y/y); server prioritized over client given scarcity; server CPU guided to grow double-digits through year-end. Intel Products op income $4.06B (CCG $2.5B + DCAI $1.5B) (10-Q). Underneath the dollar growth, x86 server **share keeps bleeding to AMD**: BofA's 4Q25 tracker shows INTC server units +9% q/q (~5.0M) but unit share down ~1.5pts q/q and AMD ASP now at a **~70% premium to Intel** in server (BofA CPU deep-dive, 2026-02-23); UBS likewise has AMD continuing to take x86 server unit and revenue share into 2026 (UBS 2026 overview, 2025-12-22).
- **Intel Foundry $5.4B** (+20% q/q) but **op loss $(2.4B)**; external third-party foundry + A&T revenue just **$174M** (10-Q) — the external book is still tiny. Management targets quarterly loss reduction through FY26 toward exit-2027 break-even.
- **Contrarian share read — Intel *gaining* x86 server share, and less constrained than AMD:** an expert from one of the largest ODMs (a CPU buyer) put Intel's x86 server share at **50% (2024) → 55% (2025) → ~60% (2026)** — a surprise vs the consensus "bleeding to AMD" narrative. The mechanism: Intel moved **laptop/desktop designs to 18A**, which lifted 18A utilization/yields *and* freed existing-node capacity for Xeon servers — explaining the surprise margin beat last quarter (even selling low-spec chips); on the supply side Intel "seems **less capacity-constrained than AMD**" (own fabs in Ireland/Israel, no plant issue) (Redburn "Conor O'Mara Asia Trip Takeaways", 2026-06-23). Server CPU pricing: both Intel and AMD raised general server CPU pricing ~15-20% earlier this year and are expected to **raise another +15-20% in H2** (Intel constrained by in-house yield, AMD by TSMC N3 utilization) (Redburn "Conor O'Mara Asia Trip Takeaways", 2026-06-23). **Confirmation (2026-07-07):** Intel has reportedly **raised prices across a range of CPUs, with data-center processors seeing the largest increases — the flagship 128-core Xeon 6980P now US$13,955, up ~12%** (TrendForce, 2026-07-07) — a concrete data point on the H2 server-CPU hike, supportive of DCAI ASP/margin.
- **CXL 3.1 CPU roadmap slips (2026-07-07):** Samsung delayed mass production of its CXL 3.1 memory module (CMM-D 3.0) **following the postponed launches of CXL 3.1-supporting CPUs from Intel and AMD** (@jukan05, 2026-07-07) — i.e., Intel's CXL 3.1-capable server CPU has pushed out, a modest negative for the CXL-memory-fabric attach story (host-CPU-gated). Read-through to [SAMSUNG](SAMSUNG.md) / [cxl-memory-fabric](themes/cxl-memory-fabric.md).
- **18A yields tracking ~2-3 months ahead of internal benchmarks**; "fastest new-product ramps in 5 years" (Xeon 6, Core Series 3 / Panther Lake) (Q1 FY26 call). **Capex flat y/y but equipment spend +25% y/y**; shell capacity already built (Arizona Fab 62, Ireland), so adds are facilitization not greenfield (StoneX, 2026-05-12). Asia channel cross-read is more cautious: US fab investment "remains weak," not increasing next year, with construction sites hit by severe labor shortages (Daifuku via Jefferies Techknowledge, 2025-12-17).
- **Guidance Q2 FY26:** revenue **$13.8–14.8B** (+2-9% q/q). BBG consensus Q2-26E (auto-injected below): ~$14.4B rev, ~39% GM, EPS ~$0.21.
- **Q4 FY25 DCAI beat, in hindsight the first sign of the inflection:** DCAI segment revenue **$4.7B** (up from **$4.1B** in Q3'25), op margin **26.4%** (up from 23.4% Q3'25 and just 8.6% Q4'24) — Intel's own reported figures. SemiAnalysis frames this as "Intel saw an unexpected uptick in datacenter CPU demand in late 2025" and is **raising 2026 capex guidance on foundry tools and prioritizing wafers to server from PC** to alleviate the supply constraint — consistent with the wafer-reallocation mechanism Redburn's ODM-expert channel-check flagged independently for Q1 FY26 (SemiAnalysis/Wong & Patel "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.2-3).
- **Diamond Rapids (2026 flagship) architecture detail — new platform "Oak Stream":** 8ch and 16ch memory variants, PCIe Gen6, built on **Intel 18A**, up to 4 compute tiles/CPU, **192 cores max, 500W TDP**, 1S/2S/4S configs, native TF32/FP8 support in AMX. Architecturally it moves to **4 Core Building Block (CBB) dies flanking two I/O and Memory Hub (IMH) dies** — a multi-NUMA-node design similar to AMD's, abandoning the large single mesh after Granite Rapids' 10×19 mesh hit scaling limits. Each CBB packs 32 Dual Core Modules on Intel 18A-P hybrid-bonded onto an Intel 3-PT base die (2 cores/L2); the full die has 256 cores but **only up to 192 are enabled for mainline SKUs** — a yield tell (see Risks). Die-to-die interconnect drops EMIB in favor of long substrate traces from each CBB to both IMH dies (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.31-33,39).
- **Rare Intel ISA lead — Advanced Performance Extensions (APX):** Diamond Rapids supports APX, which AMD's Zen7/Venice does not. AMD CTO Mark Papermaster has confirmed Zen7's new matrix-multiplication engine and AVX10/FRED/ChkTag will have debuted first on Intel Diamond Rapids — a rare case of Intel leading on an ISA feature, a counterweight to the otherwise bearish Diamond Rapids framing below (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.64).
- **Intel now reporting gains from its OpenAI/Mistral investments** — "having languished for decades, [Intel] has recovered toward valuation levels last seen at the height of the dot-com boom" and "has reported making money from its investments in OpenAI and Mistral" (Barron's "Other Voices," 2026-06-08).
- **Apple foundry commitment — WSJ (2026-07-11):** [[AAPL]] reportedly secured an **exemption from semiconductor tariffs in exchange for committing to use Intel's fabs** — and will manufacture **not only Mac chips but also iPhone chips at Intel** (WSJ, 2026-07-11; @jukan05, 2026-07-11). Escalates the mid-June "Apple chip deal" (which drove INTC **+8-9% on 06-18**) and the "M7 on 18A-P" thread into a materially bigger external-foundry commitment spanning Apple's highest-volume product (iPhone) — potentially the single largest validation of the Intel Foundry thesis if it holds, though **node/wafer-volume/timing are unspecified** and prior Apple-Intel foundry reports drew skepticism (Fubon read Apple as leveraging TSMC on capacity, not a genuine switch — see Catalysts/AAPL page).

## Debate / thesis
- **Bull:**
  - **18A/14A is finally executing.** 18A yields ahead of internal plan; 14A "maturity, yield and performance outpacing 18A" at the equivalent point, with external customers engaged in the *definition* phase (vs only development for 18A) and management landing more internal tiles on 14A — a confidence tell (StoneX 2026-05-12; Q1 FY26 call). ASML 18A/14A commentary on customer wins / capacity-ramp needs is one of the pillars of the bullish semicap trade (BofA/Evans via briefing, 2026-06-15). Process-architecture edge: Intel is the **first-mover on backside-power (BSPDN)**, shipping at end-2025 vs TSMC late-2026/early-2027 and Samsung 2027 — though it chose the simpler **Power Via** implementation while TSMC/Samsung took Direct Backside Contact, which Bernstein argues should give TSMC a performance advantage despite later adoption (Bernstein Semis deck, May-2025).
  - **Packaging as the trojan horse.** EMIB/EMIB-T reticle ~30% larger than CoWoS; per-engagement value re-rated from "hundreds of millions" to **multiple billions/yr**; backlog growing, ramp visible 2027 (StoneX 2026-05-12). Redburn (bus tour, briefing 2026-06-17): Intel "put on a brave face" but advanced packaging is the real play — "using packaging as a trojan horse into customer wins." Asia checks sharpen the call to **EMIB-T, not plain EMIB**: **Google is evaluating EMIB-T for TPU v9 and Meta for next-gen MTIA**, citing CoWoS capacity constraints and EMIB-T's power-delivery advantages in the HBM4 era; Intel CVP John Pitzer guided EMIB-T to a **"meaningful contribution in 2H26"** with the narrative shifting from CoWoS overflow to technology-driven adoption (Jefferies Techknowledge, 2025-12-17). Optical optionality sits on top: Intel's CPO roadmap pairs an **OCI chiplet (4 Tbit/s bi-directional, 64 lanes @ 32 Gbit/s, ~5 pJ/bit) co-packaged with a Xeon CPU**, a 2025 detachable glass optical-bridge connector, and a 2027 target for 3D-integrated (vertical) photonics (Semianalysis CPO primer, 2026-02).
    - **Update — EMIB-T now load-bearing for an external customer's roadmap, not just an Intel bet.** Morgan Stanley sales (Amir Amerian, "glass/panels" note) telegraphs performance/cost advantages of EMIB-T over TSMC's CoWoS specifically for chips **above 9.7 reticle-size**, driven by (1) higher sample yields and (2) a warpage/cost edge at large chip sizes (Morgan Stanley · Amir Amerian, 2026-06-30).
    - **ECTC 2026 — the technical case for EMIB-T, from Intel's own disclosures.** Intel was the **largest corporate presenter at ECTC** (12 papers vs TSMC's 3, Samsung's 11), and SemiAnalysis calls EMIB-T (next-gen EMIB **with TSVs**) the **"most credible alternative to TSMC's CoWoS platform for large-package AI accelerators,"** with the disclosures explaining **why it is expected to be used in Google's TPU v9**. Specifics: validated at **36/35 µm bump pitch on a 2× reticle package** (down from 45 µm on Granite Rapids = 65% higher bump density), with 36/35 µm now expanding to **4.5× reticle silicon (cert targeted end-2026)** and a **25 µm pitch** test vehicle underway; a **240×240 mm quarter-panel** test vehicle (~67 reticles) shown (though the booth sample had "severe warpage"). The "T" is for the **TSVs** that deliver power vertically through the bridge — Intel claims **DC voltage drop cut 68-80%**; on-bridge MIM caps (500 nF/mm², ~Intel-18A-class) improve PDN AC impedance **>82%**; simulated **HBM4E at 12 Gb/s** held **>60% UI eye width** to 16 Gb/s. The forward roadmap adds active bridges, embedded voltage regulators, and >2500 nF/mm² eMIM-T caps. SemiAnalysis's caveat (see Bear): EMIB-T is **"still behind TSMC's CoWoS on several vectors"** (TSMC already deploys DTC/eDTC, is further along on IVR and active LSI) — "Intel is still catching up to an ecosystem that has been executing in volume for years." Separately, Intel showed an **industry-first 510×515 mm, 24-layer glass-core panel** (copper-filled TGVs, 2 embedded EMIB bridges, co-formed optical waveguides, no SeWaRe cracking after thermal shock) — a leading data point in the glass-substrate race vs TSMC (SemiAnalysis "EMIB-T Roadmap / ECTC 2026", 2026-07-02). The sharpest confirmation: JPMorgan's MediaTek note ties **upside in MediaTek's TPU v9 ("Humufish") unit** partly to the **progress of Intel's EMIB-T packaging yield** — i.e. EMIB-T yield is now a modeled input to a major external customer's accelerator roadmap, not just an internal Intel packaging bet (JPMorgan · Gokul Hariharan, MediaTek note, 2026-07-01). UBS corroborates the traction but flags a capacity/prioritization catch: EMIB-T is **resource/capacity constrained in 2028**, and Intel will likely prioritize internal products plus the Google/MediaTek TPU v9 engagements first — i.e. real demand, but a rationing problem, not a green light for everyone (UBS · Sunny Lin, 2026-07-01).
    - **Bear-side nuance on the same engagement — yield is the gate (Fubon, 2026-07-08).** Fubon's latest checks confirm **Google initially planned to use Intel EMIB-T for its TPU V9 production in late-2027/2028** — a positive advanced-packaging read-through that Intel Foundry's packaging trojan-horse is landing on a marquee external roadmap. But Google **asked Intel to lift EMIB-T yield to 95%+ before production**, and Fubon sees some yield improvement yet judges hitting that target by mid-2027 "very challenging," concluding Google will "at last rely on TSMC's [2028 CoWoS] expansion plan." Net: the design intent validates EMIB-T's competitiveness, but the yield bar (echoing Fubon's earlier 60-70% EMIB-vs-95%+-CoWoS framing, 2026-06-05) keeps the 2028 volume outcome contested (Fubon "TSMC — Expecting GM upside going forward", 2026-07-08).
  - **CPU-per-GPU re-rating + supply scarcity.** Tan frames the ratio moving from ~1:8 (training) → 1:4 (inference) → parity (agentic); NVIDIA host-node win (custom Xeon in NVLink for Rubin NVL8) levels the field vs Arm. Server is supply-, not speeds-and-feeds-, constrained today — an IDM with internal wafers has more levers, and tightness likely extends into 2027 (StoneX 2026-05-12). BofA frames the same TAM tailwind: **server CPU TAM ~$27B (CY25) → ~$60B (CY30E), +17% CAGR**, with AI servers ~70% of that and CPUs gaining inference relevance — Intel's *dollar* sales likely still grow even as share falls (BofA CPU deep-dive, 2026-02-23).
  - **Recapitalized balance sheet.** Cash/STI ~$37.4B exiting FY25 after **NVIDIA $5B** (215M shares @ $23.28), **SoftBank $2B** (87M @ $23.00), **US government** (CHIPS Secure Enclave; up to 433M shares to DOC @ $20.47 + 241M warrants @ $20), Altera 51% ($4.3B) and Mobileye monetizations (10-K FY25; Q3/Q4 FY25 calls).
  - **The capex turn / "Intel Should Raise Capital" thesis.** SemiAnalysis (Patel/Kundojjala) frames Q1-26 as the inflection: Intel raised FY26 capex guide to flat y/y (from flat-to-down) — the **first capex guide-up in the saga** — and bought back **Apollo's 49% of Fab 34 for $14.2B** (closed Apr-8-2026, ~$7.7B cash + $6.5B bridge), management calling the SCIP unwind accretive, i.e. conceding the off-balance-sheet structures were expensive money. With ~$51.5B of debt post-bridge and the alternatives (SCIPs, more debt, asset sales) exhausted, SemiAnalysis argues equity is now Intel's cheapest capital and it should raise **~$40B** (~4-5% dilution off a ~$498B mkt cap) to fund the **Terafab** ramp (SpaceX/Tesla anchor; multi-phase up to **$119B**, 100k→1M WSPM) and the **N3-overflow opportunity** — if Agentic-CPU TAM is ~$200bn (~100k WSPM) and most N3 shifts to accelerators, **~50k+ WSPM could flow to Intel** as TSMC stays capex-conservative; it pegs a **$20-35B financing hole**. The order book is filling: NVIDIA DGX Rubin NVL8 lists dual Xeon 6 host CPUs, Google signed a multiyear Xeon + custom-IPU deal, SambaNova on inference (SemiAnalysis "Intel Should Raise Capital", 2026-06-11; building on its Dec-2024 "Intel on the Brink of Death" board-failure thesis). Funding/dilution risk is the obvious flip side — see Bear.
  - **Apple as a foundry customer — potentially the marquee external win.** WSJ (2026-07-11) reports [[AAPL]] secured a **semiconductor-tariff exemption in exchange for committing to Intel's fabs**, extending beyond Mac chips to **iPhone chips at Intel** — if it materializes at scale, exactly the "first major external customer" the whole foundry thesis is gated on, and on Apple's highest-volume product. Follows the mid-June Apple-chip-deal sentiment pop (INTC +8-9%, 06-18) and the M7-on-18A-P reports. Caveat: node/volume/timeline unspecified, and skeptics read prior Apple-Intel foundry noise as capacity leverage over TSMC rather than a real switch (Fubon, 2026-06-05). (WSJ, 2026-07-11; @jukan05, 2026-07-11)
- **Bear:**
  - **Foundry still loses ~$2.4B/qtr and the external book is $174M.** Break-even is an *exit-2027* aspiration that management has repeatedly caveated will slip the moment a big customer is won (you layer cost ahead of revenue) (StoneX 2026-05-12). Long-run target is only ~40% GM / ~30% OM for external wafers & packaging — structurally below Intel's historical product margins, and ASICs/packaging/external wafers all dilute corporate GM as they scale (StoneX 2026-05-12).
  - **Structural server-share erosion to AMD/ARM.** BofA models INTC server value share falling from **63% (CY24) toward ~40% (CY30E)** while AMD goes 33%→~40% and **ARM 5%→20%+** (toward 25%+ if more workloads shift to custom Arm silicon) — the loss is mostly at Intel's expense; ARM already claims ~50% of *new* hyperscale instances and NVIDIA's standalone **Vera Arm CPU (2H26)** is a fresh merchant entrant into Intel's enterprise turf, with the main offset being x86's still-dominant ~80-90% installed-base ecosystem in CY26E (BofA CPU deep-dive, 2026-02-23; Jefferies Techknowledge 2025-12-17). EMIB itself is contested: Kinsus calls plain EMIB **too expensive with very low yield and hard to mass-produce**, and sees IC designers preferring to wait for TSMC to expand CoWoS (Jefferies Techknowledge, 2025-12-17). Fubon's checks reinforce this — **EMIB production yield is only ~60-70% vs TSMC CoWoS 95%+**, and EMIB-T "is not yet ready to enter mass production," so MediaTek/Google's priority stays TSMC CoWoS-L despite the EMIB-T interest (Fubon, 2026-06-05). On servers, UBS independently models Intel's **server-CPU unit share falling 60% (2025) → 29% (2030)** as ARM rises 16%→42% and AMD 24%→29% (UBS server-CPU TAM, 2026-05-21) — directionally the same erosion BofA flagged on a value-share basis.
  - **Roadmap still behind on speeds-and-feeds.** Management concedes Diamond Rapids' lack of SMT was "a mistake" (8-channel non-hyperscale SKU pulled); real competitiveness vs AMD waits for **Coral Rapids**, with no firm date. Near-term share stabilization is a who-gets-more-wafers story, not a competitiveness story (StoneX 2026-05-12).
  - **The SMT gap, quantified — the single most important competitive datapoint on Diamond Rapids.** Intel began removing simultaneous multi-threading (hyper-threading) starting with Lion Cove (2024 client PC) — a reasonable trade for PCs given integrated E-cores, but "maximum throughput matters for datacenter CPUs, severely handicapping Diamond Rapids." SemiAnalysis's math: versus the current **128-core/256-thread Granite Rapids**, the new **192-core/192-thread Diamond Rapids will be only around 40% faster**, "exposing Intel for another generation with lower performance than AMD." Because AMD already gets significantly higher performance per core (96-core Turin matches 128-core Granite Rapids), "the performance gap between AMD Venice and Intel Diamond Rapids will widen even more in the 2026 to 2028 generation." Cross-CBB latency is a further overhang: SemiAnalysis "expect[s] cross-CBB latencies to be appreciably worse off" now that die-to-die links run over long substrate traces rather than EMIB (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.32-33,36).
  - **Show-me on the external customer.** A former Marvell AVP was "skeptical about customer shift to Intel for leading-edge nodes — yield/delay risks too high" (AlphaSense expert via briefing, 2026-06-17). 14A funding is explicitly gated on a binding external commitment that has not yet been disclosed (Q2 FY25 call, 2025-07-24).
  - **Dilution + GAAP losses.** Share count jumped to ~5,083M (from 4,343M y/y) on the SoftBank/NVIDIA/DOC issuances; GAAP EPS $(0.73) on restructuring; 10-K flags a potential *pause/discontinuation of 14A and next-gen nodes* if no significant external customer materializes.
  - **"The foundry upside is already in the market cap" (BofA's Vivek Arya).** On a CPU-roadmap call, Arya argued INTC's market cap rose **~$110B since the prior earnings call**, conceptually already anticipating near-perfect foundry execution: at his model's **$3B (2027) / $6B (2028) external foundry revenue**, applying TSMC's ~10x price-to-sales values the external book at **~$60B** (~$100B if external revenue is $10B) — so the move already discounts that. He pegs **best-case earnings power at only ~$2–2.50**, and notes that adding back stock-comp expense would **cut reported EPS by ~30–50%** on an apples-to-apples basis vs NVDA/AVGO/AMD (BofA "CPU roadmap" call, 2026-04-22).
  - **The model doesn't work below mid-40s GM.** Arya frames the gating economics bluntly: to be a viable manufacturer Intel's capital intensity must run **~30–35%** (vs TSMC's 40–50%), and **without sustainable mid-40s gross margin** it cannot translate sales into meaningful EPS or cash — "the toughest thing for the company to do." Near-term GM upside is real but quality-flagged: the **server-CPU growth is mostly ASP, not units** (Q1 units roughly **flat y/y**), and the offset is that holding PC share forces **more wafers from TSMC at higher cost** plus the 18A yield drag (BofA "CPU roadmap" call, 2026-04-22).

## Risks
- **No external 14A customer → potential pause/discontinuation of 14A and next-gen leading-edge nodes** (explicit 10-K risk factor).
- **Foundry losses persist**; break-even slips when a big customer is layered on; ~40/30 endpoint GM/OM structurally below legacy product margins; ASIC/packaging/external-wafer mix dilutive to corporate GM.
- **Competitive position vs AMD/Arm** in servers still gated on Coral Rapids; Diamond Rapids SMT misstep; hyperscaler BYO (internal Arm) silicon a structural share headwind in DCAI; BofA sees server value share toward ~40% by CY30E (BofA CPU deep-dive, 2026-02-23).
- **Sierra Forest — direct hyperscaler-rejection evidence.** SemiAnalysis reports hyperscaler adoption of the 144-core E-core Sierra Forest Xeon "has been limited" because hyperscalers are "already adopting AMD and designing their own ARM-based CPUs," while traditional enterprise customers "were not interested" either. The dual-die 288-core Sierra Forest-AP (Xeon 6900E) never reached general availability and survives only as a low-volume, off-roadmap part — concrete, already-realized share loss rather than a forward risk (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.25).
- **Clearwater Forest's successor line is confirmed dead.** SemiAnalysis titles a section "Clearwater Forest Failure" (p.25); its E-core successor (codenamed Rouge River Forest / DMR-HD) has been cancelled under Lip-Bu Tan's roadmap streamlining — "the series will end with just 2 generations" (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.65-66).
- **Diamond Rapids-SP (mainstream 8-channel) cancelled outright — a multi-year product-line gap.** In "a late move," Intel cancelled the mainstream Diamond Rapids-SP platform entirely, "leaving their highest volume core market without a new generation into at least 2028." SemiAnalysis calls this "the wrong move," arguing AI tool-use/agentic context-storage workloads favor mainstream memory connectivity over max performance-per-socket flagship parts — i.e. Intel is skipping a generation in the segment where volume (and share defense) actually lives (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.33).
- **Diamond Rapids yield read-through.** The full CBB die has 256 cores but mainline SKUs enable only up to 192 — a level of core disablement that reads as a yield/binning constraint rather than a clean architectural choice (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.32).
- **Cross-CBB latency.** Dropping EMIB for long substrate traces between each Core Building Block die and the two I/O/Memory-Hub dies removes an advanced-packaging cost step but SemiAnalysis explicitly flags "cross-CBB latencies to be appreciably worse off" — a potential real-workload performance tax not captured in the headline core-count/TDP specs (SemiAnalysis "CPUs are Back," 2026-02-09, p.32-33).
- **Execution / yield** — 18A still below management's bar; "7-8% yield improvement/month" but not yet at industry standard.
- **PC TAM down low-double-digits FY26** on memory-cost-driven inflation hitting sell-through; supply-constrained on older nodes (Intel 10/7).
- **Government/partner overhang** — DOC equity (up to 433M shares) + warrants (241M @ $20) contingent on Intel keeping ≥51% of the foundry; NVIDIA/SoftBank/DOC issuance diluted share count ~+17% y/y; geopolitical/trade and China exposure.
- **Valuation** — at ~72x NTM PE the stock already discounts meaningful foundry success; downside if the external-customer / 18A-yield narrative stalls (UBS 2026 overview, 2025-12-22).
- **Note on financials:** Intel's reported EPS is **near-breakeven** on a normalized basis; GAAP is swung by large restructuring/impairment charges and equity-investment gains/losses — do not read the GAAP loss as run-rate.
