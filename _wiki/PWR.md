## Snapshot
Quanta is the leading US/Canada/Australia infrastructure-solutions contractor for electric & gas utilities, power generation, large load centers (data centers), pipelines and communications — design, engineering, procurement, construction, upgrade and repair/maintenance (10-K 2026-02-19). As of the 2025 10-K it reports **two** segments: **Electric Infrastructure Solutions** (the old Electric Power + Renewable Energy segments combined) and **Underground Utility & Infrastructure Solutions** (10-K 2026-02-19). The model is decentralized — multiple operating companies, ~69,500 employees (Q4 FY25, 2026-02-19). The thesis is a single word: it is the labor- and execution-layer pick-and-shovel on the once-in-a-generation US grid build-out — transmission, electrification, gas backup, renewables/storage and data-center power. Data center work is now ~10% of business and the fastest-growing slice of backlog (Q4 FY25, 2026-02-19).

## Current state (latest quarter)
**Q1 FY26 (call 2026-04-30):** revenue $7.9bn, adjusted EBITDA $686m, adjusted diluted EPS $2.68 — all double-digit growth; **record backlog $48.5bn** (CFO Desai). Mgmt **raised** FY26 guide: revenue $34.7–35.2bn, adj. EBITDA $3.49–3.65bn, adj. EPS **$13.55–14.25**; FCF guide unchanged with confidence at the "higher end" (Desai). Earl Austin: backlog growth was "broad-based" — the 765 kV transmission project was "less than 25%" / "less than $1bn" of the increase, so not single-project dependent; expects backlog to "continue to rise." Achieved "30% EPS growth with no acquisitions in the quarter" (Austin). Newly announced **$500–700m multi-year investment** in high-voltage (345–765 kV) power-transformer manufacturing — meant to attack the "36 months on transformers" lead-time bottleneck and de-risk customers like AEP via a US-based supply chain (Austin). Off-site manufacturing/logistics footprint ~6.7m sq ft. NiSource/Alphabet GenCo opportunity cited at "$5.7bn…growing every day," with announced CCGT/generation work **not yet in backlog** (air permits pending; expected "later half of the year and beyond").

**FY25 context (Q4 call 2026-02-19):** record FY revenue $28.5bn (+20%), adj. EBITDA $2.9bn, adj. EPS $10.75 (+20%), FCF $1.7bn; 8 acquisitions / ~11,100 employees added. Year-end backlog $44bn. Framing target: **>2x earnings power by 2030**, i.e. a **15–20% adj. EPS CAGR** through 2030 (Austin/Desai, both calls).

## Debate / thesis
- **Bull:**
  - **Multi-decade demand, not a single cycle.** Austin: it took "70 or 100 years to build the grid…you can't double it overnight"; CCGT orders run into 2030 → execution into "2033 and beyond"; demand lasts "a decade plus" (Q1 FY26, 2026-04-30). Even discounting the doubling thesis "50%, it's still doubling the largest infrastructure project in the world"; visibility to 2032 (Q4 FY25, 2026-02-19).
  - **Mega-bull free-rider.** BofA / Fenske places PWR inside the AI mega-bull framework as one of the cycle's "free-rider winners" alongside the hyperscalers (briefing 2026-05-11). Read-throughs flagged: GEV, NVDA-power, BE/AAOI.
  - **AI = incremental catalyst on a structurally underinvested grid (independent buy-side validation).** A Barron's tech roundtable expert names PWR a top pick — "a leader in transmission and distribution infrastructure" — arguing AI is "one additional catalyst for power-demand growth in the U.S. after decades of power demand barely growing," that "the grid has been underinvested in for decades," and that "the market is massively underestimating the investment cycle there, and Quanta's leadership position" (Barron's Tech Roundtable, 2026-03-28; ref. price PWR $260.64 as of 2025-03-26). Corroborates the same T&D / electrification thesis mgmt frames, from an outside investor — not a sell-side rating.
  - **Labor + supply-chain moat compounding.** "Labor builds labor" — added 5,000–6,000 organically last year, repeat + M&A on top (Austin, Q1 FY26). Vertical integration into transformer manufacturing turns a competitor bottleneck into a Quanta advantage; shift to "5-, 10-year programmatic spend" vs. one-off bids lifts ROIC (Austin, Q4 FY25).
  - **Broad-based, not one mega-project.** "There is no SunZia" driving backlog (Austin, Q4 FY25) — quality of growth is high.
- **Bear:**
  - **Indirect, not a sponsored call.** The only desk datapoint on disk is a *thematic* mention — "No active move… indirect read-through from hyperscaler capex theme" (briefing 2026-05-11). No PT, no rating in archive; bull case rests on mgmt + theme.
  - **Margin ceiling / regulated-customer constraint.** Austin repeatedly steers to "compounding" value over margin-% and cites regulated-utility constraints (Q4 FY25); Underground "has the ability to operate in double digits" only "over time" (Q1 FY26).
  - **CCGT risk allocation.** Refuses "firm fixed price generation" on combined cycle; will be "prudent," "won't win them all," and won't grow the org beyond the market (Austin, both calls) — caps the gas-gen upside that bulls extrapolate.
  - **Lumpy big-ticket awards.** No significant 765 kV in backlog at Q4; major awards not expected until "late half of 2027" then over 3–5 years (Austin, Q4 FY25) — air pocket risk on the biggest projects.
- **Where the sell-side/buy-side stands:** thin on disk. **BofA/Fenske** (2026-05-11) thematic mega-bull inclusion; **Jefferies/Rankie Wong** (2026-05-11) lists PWR in a broad US-infrastructure basket; **Barron's roundtable** (2026-03-28) carries PWR as an expert top pick on the T&D / AI-power thesis. The one archived rating: **Bernstein (Chad Dillard) rates PWR Market-Perform, PT $538** (vs price $587.42, i.e. modest downside on valuation), EPS 2025A $10.75 / **2026E $13.04 / 2027E $15.32** (P/E ~45x 2026E / ~38x 2027E) — and, in a Section-232 metals-tariff note, flags **service companies (URI, LGN, J, PWR) as "the path of least resistance"** to play the tariff theme, being relatively insulated vs. the ag/construction/electrical-equipment OEMs that carry the metals-cost exposure (Bernstein "Machinery & Electricals: The Section 232 metals tariff…", 2026-04-16). Sell-side coverage breadth is wide on the calls (Evercore, Citi, UBS, Jefferies, GS, KeyBanc, Stifel, Wolfe, Baird, Truist, Bernstein, Vertical, Mizuho, JPM, Cantor) but stances are otherwise not captured here.
- **Expert-call read-through — labor is the binding constraint (the PWR moat).** Two DC-buildout expert calls reinforce the labor thesis that anchors PWR's >2x-earnings-by-2030 case. SinMax/IIR's build-tracking work pegs the gating factor as **craft labor — electricians, pipefitters, welders, HVAC** — with **5 retiring for every 1 entrant** and ~1M total US electricians (1–10 needed per MW); their EPC outliers on power construction are **Kiewit, TIC, Burns & McDonnell** (data-center EPC: Turner, Fluor, DPR, AECOM, Jacobs) — PWR/Quanta not named directly, but the labor scarcity is precisely what insulates a self-perform labor aggregator (Jefferies "DC build / Vulcan-IIR", 2026-06-22). The DC broker likewise calls **power the #1 gating factor** and craft labor (electricians) the deepest shortage (JPM DC broker call, 2026-05-04). Read as corroboration of "labor builds labor" — supportive of PWR's pick-and-shovel thesis, not a rating.

- **Transformer-manufacturing ramp** — execution against the $500–700m investment; lead-time relief is the de-risking story for AEP and large-load customers.
- **765 kV transmission award cadence** — major awards skewed to late-2027+ (Q4 FY25); permitting reform a swing factor (Austin).
- **M&A** — Austin expects deals "over the next 9 months" and "2 or 3 years," additive to (not in) current guidance (Q1 FY26).

## Risks
- Fixed-price and non-fixed-price construction/engineering contracts — cost-overrun/execution exposure (10-K 2026-02-19).
- Dependence on **skilled labor / experienced operators**; craft-labor tightness, especially data-center work, the "tightest market," with transmission and fiber-splicer roles tightening next (Austin, Q4 FY25; 10-K 2026-02-19).
- Acquisition-integration risk — 8 deals / ~11,100 employees added in FY25 alone (Q4 FY25).
- Customer/end-market concentration in regulated utilities and a small set of large-load (hyperscaler) customers; regulated-customer pricing constrains margin.
- Project/award lumpiness on big transmission and CCGT work; permitting and air-permit timing.
- Commodity/geopolitical noise — mgmt says diesel is "a little piece" and guide "contemplates" such moves (Austin, Q1 FY26), but it remains a swing on input costs.
