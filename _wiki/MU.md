## Snapshot
Micron is one of three scaled DRAM makers (with Samsung and SK Hynix) and a top-tier NAND supplier, selling DRAM/NAND/NOR under the Micron and Crucial brands (FY25 10-K, 2025-10-03). DRAM is ~79% of revenue, NAND ~20-21% (Q2 FY26, 2026-03-18). The franchise is reorganized around four data-center-centric BUs: Cloud Memory (CMBU), Core Data Center (CDBU), Mobile & Client (MCBU), and Auto & Embedded (AEBU). The thesis hinges on memory being repositioned "from a system component to a strategic asset" for AI — Micron supplies the full memory hierarchy that agentic inference consumes: HBM next to the accelerator, LPDDR/DDR5/SoCAM expansion memory on the CPU, and high-capacity QLC/TLC data-center SSDs for the growing KV-cache/context tier (Sathya Kumar via UBS Arcuri call, 2026-05-15). It is the #2-3 HBM player behind SK Hynix; only credible third source on HBM4 given Samsung's product issues.

## HBM technology / share (deep dive)
- **MU's HBM positioning:** Micron leapfrogged in HBM — it skipped standard HBM3 entirely — on the back of a focus on TSVs and the power-delivery network, underpinning its claimed ~30% lower power consumption (claim **explicitly unverified** — SemiAnalysis flags this as an unverified claim, not confirmed) (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall," 2025-08-11, p.7-8). Micron and Samsung use Non-Conductive Film (NCF) / thermal-compression bonding (TCB) vs Hynix's proprietary MR-MUF molded underfill (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall," 2025-08-11, p.6). For MU and SK, yield loss on HBM is more than offset by pricing → HBM is margin-accretive; Samsung's worse yields ironically tighten total supply (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall," 2025-08-11, p.8).
- **HBM4 node choice:** Micron is staying on the **1b node for HBM4** — the same node as SK Hynix — rather than jumping to a new node the way Samsung is attempting with 1c (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall," 2025-08-11, p.47). Micron **lacks internal advanced-logic design capability** and needs silicon partners/TSMC for the HBM4 custom base die (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall," 2025-08-11, p.26) — consistent with the base-die logic-gap point below.
- **AMD supply-share shift:** AMD has shifted a significant share of its HBM3E supply **to Micron and away from Samsung** (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall," 2025-08-11, p.46) — a customer-level share data point supporting MU's #2-3 HBM positioning independent of the TAM/pricing commentary elsewhere on this page.
- **HBM4 base die — the key watch item:** the HBM4 base die moves to a modern logic process (TSMC N12 and N3 variants). Pure-memory makers Hynix and Micron lack leading-edge logic design capability and will lean on TSMC + IP partners; only Samsung can build the advanced base die in-house. Custom base dies arrive with HBM4 for NVIDIA/AMD, broadening to other accelerators at HBM4E (~2027); they strengthen vendor lock-in because each is tied to a vendor's proprietary TSV layout (SemiAnalysis, 2025-08-12). Caution flag: Jefferies expert (Inspectrum, 2025-12-17) says **Micron is redesigning its HBM4 base die** to reach speed parity — the two Korean players (esp. Samsung) carry higher confidence on higher speeds, i.e. MU has work to do on the new base die.
- **HBM4 pin-speed execution risk — SemiAnalysis skepticism (net-new, sharper than the Jefferies caution above):** MU's HBM4 samples showed **"much lower pin speeds"** than Samsung/SK Hynix (~10 Gbps); SemiAnalysis is explicitly **"skeptical of execution"** despite management's public confidence, and forecasts **~0% Micron share of Rubin HBM supply in the first 12 months** (SemiAnalysis "Memory Mania," 2026-02-06, p.29). This is a materially more cautious read than the "redesigning for speed parity" framing already on this page and should be weighed as the more bearish end of the HBM4-execution range.
- **Fab-timeline detail (SemiAnalysis "Memory Mania," 2026-02-06):** the **A3 fab (Idaho)** is predominantly HBM-allocated; its ramp has been pulled in from 2H27 to **mid-2027**, with wafer output more likely **3Q27** (p.23, 30). The newly acquired **P5 Tongluo fab** (acquired from PSMC) is unlikely to show meaningful output until **2H27**; max capacity ~**45k wafers/month** for commodity DRAM at full utilization, potentially lower if configured for HBM (p.23, 30). SemiAnalysis expects **~30% of DRAM production on the 1c/1γ node by end-2026** (p.24), and pegs **2026 DRAM WFE capex growth at +20%** — the lowest of the three memory makers' capex growth rates (p.34).
- **HBM share (UBS estimates, 2026 overview):** MU HBM bit shipment 0.9 / 4.6 / 7.4 bn Gb (2024/25/26E); HBM as % of MU total DRAM bits 1.5% / 6.2% / 8.4%; MU HBM bit-share of the market ~21-22% in 2026E (vs SK Hynix the leader). MU HBM front-end capacity 30 / 65 / 100 k-wpm (4Q, '24/'25/'26E). Samsung's 2025 HBM share ~20%, "will go up next year" (Jefferies/Inspectrum, 2025-12-17). UBS: MU's HBM business alone supports **$5 / $7+ of EPS in 2026/2027** (~17-18% of EPS), justifying a higher multiple on the segment.

- **Agentic-AI memory-hierarchy sizing (UBS teach-in with MU mgmt, 2026-06-08):** a single agentic prompt can spawn ~4-5 simultaneous agents (each a separate CPU task interfacing with GPU/ASIC), and **query context grows ~30x/yr** — a multiplicative step-up in DRAM capacity, HBM bandwidth, and SSD storage per query. MU quantified the tiers: **HBM-resident active KV cache ~10-100GB/GPU at ~1-20TB/s** (residency ms-to-s); next tier **~200-400GB KV cache/GPU on DRAM at ~100-500GB/s** (s-to-min); **NAND "context memory" tens-to-hundreds of TB** (warm data / vector DB). Products: 192GB SOCAMM already shipping in volume + newly announced 256GB SOCAMM and 256GB RDIMM (**~3x DRAM capacity per CPU socket** vs prior gen); 245TB SSD (rack-level → multi-petabyte AI storage). MU's stance on efficiency (TurboQuant, sparse attention, quantization): gains get reinvested into larger context windows / more agents rather than cutting hardware content (UBS "FQ3:26 preview / agentic teach-in", 2026-06-08). UBS's $1,625 PT = **~15x NTM P/E on C2029E EPS ~$124-125, discounted back** (C2029 is UBS's through-cycle anchor, assuming a moderate downcycle buoyed by LTAs).

## Debate / thesis
- **LTA/SCA structural-shift thesis — flagged explicitly as a major bull/bear debate item.** Bulls (BNP Paribas' Ackerman via Barron's, 2026-06-29; sell-side broadly, see below) read the 16 take-or-pay contracts (~40% of revenue once fully executed, locked through 2030) as evidence the memory industry itself is "undergoing a transformative shift toward long-term supply agreements which supports demand assurance, through-cycle profitability, and mitigated cyclicality" — i.e. it de-risks the cyclicality that has always capped memory-stock multiples. Bears/skeptics flag the *newness* of the dynamic as the risk, not the reassurance: a SemiAnalysis reader comment (Drew Van Pelt, "Memory Mania," 2026-02-06, p.35 — explicitly a comment, not the SemiAnalysis analyst body) notes the current LTAs are "very different from the past," i.e. a structural break from history that the market/analysts have limited precedent to model — cuts both ways: it could mean the cyclicality is genuinely gone, or it could mean investors are underwriting duration/terms that have never been tested through a real downturn. MU's own Chief Business Officer Sumit Sadana concedes the communication gap directly: "We are putting a lot of effort into not only doing these [long-term agreements] but communicating a lot of the details, and it'll take investors some time to understand all the details" (Sadana to Barron's, 2026-06-29) — i.e. even management frames this as a still-developing narrative, not a fully priced-in fact. **Bernstein (Mark Newman) "New Memory LTAs part 2 - Case studies from Microchip" (2026-07-09)** puts the sharpest historical frame on the deposits-as-downside-protection question: Micron backs its memory-LTA obligations with **$18B of cash deposits on its own balance sheet plus $4B in letters of credit**, and — because these upfront guarantees are back-end-weighted, with the **coverage ratio rising toward 75-100% of remaining obligations in the outer years** — they give **"meaningful but not infinite" downside protection**, materially better-structured than the old semiconductor LTAs that broke through prior downturns (Microchip's PSP, Hemlock polysilicon). This is the constructive counter to the "deposits = vendor financing" bear point (@PatrickMoorhead) and the "never tested through a real downturn" skeptic read (Bernstein "New Memory LTAs part 2", 2026-07-09).
- **Bernstein "Memory LTA effectiveness" — the skeptic's quantitative bound on the same LTA debate (Bernstein · Mark Newman, 2026-07-09).** A separate, same-day Bernstein cut (distinct from the constructive "part 2 — Case studies from Microchip" note above; both Newman, 2026-07-09) puts the harder numbers on *how much* protection the deposits actually buy — quantifying the "not infinite" side of the "meaningful but not infinite" conclusion.
