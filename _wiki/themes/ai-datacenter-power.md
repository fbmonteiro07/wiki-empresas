# Theme — AI Data-Center Power & Nuclear

## What it is / why it matters

The AI build-out has turned electricity into the binding constraint on compute. Hyperscaler capex is running toward a **~$1.2T buy-side bogey for 2027** ("could be 1.3, could be 1.4," Jefferies power/hyperscalers, 2026-05-08) — and a data center is useless without firm, 24/7 power to plug into. Cantor frames the supply imbalance bluntly: NVIDIA can ship accelerators faster than anyone can build the buildings to house them — **~40 GW of chips into the US between 2025–2030 vs. ~21 GW of data center capacity built over the prior 25 years** (Cantor/CoreWeave, 2026-06-18). For a buy-sider, this is the rare theme where the bottleneck is physical — turbines, transformers, interconnects, baseload electrons, and the labor to assemble them — so the money concentrates in whoever owns scarce, contractable supply: nuclear/merchant generation (CEG, VST, TLN), gas-turbine OEMs (GEV), EPC labor (PWR, AGX), and gas midstream (WMB). The single biggest risk to the bull case is not demand — it is the grid, the labor pool, and timing.

## State of play (latest)

- **Demand is staggering and accelerating, not peaking.** Microsoft backlog +99% y/y to $627B; Oracle "half a trillion"; CoreWeave "pulled a Wayne Gretzky" at $99B backlog and declared itself sold out; 2026 hyperscaler capex is set to *accelerate* across the board (Jefferies, 2026-05-08). VST cites hyperscaler capex **>$700B in 2026, ~+50% y/y** (VST Q4 FY25, 2026-02-26); CEG calls hyperscaler 2026 capex "almost 75% higher than last year" (CEG Q1 FY26, 2026-05-11).
- **JPM's "AI Capex 2.0" sizes the wall: $5.5T through 2030.** JPM lifts total AI capex to **$5.5T through 2030** (from $5.1T), of which **$4.1T debt-financed** (LTCs averaging >85%, some >90% — "90% Loan-to-Cost ≈ 60% Loan-to-Value"). Top-4 US hyperscalers (GOOG/AMZN/MSFT/META) guided ~**$700-725B total 2026 capex, +~75% y/y** off ~$410B in 2025 (3rd straight 60%+ year); combined five-name FY26 capex >$750B, **approaching/exceeding $1.1T in FY27**, vs combined FY27 OCF ~$900B+ — so debt/equity issuance stays structural. Capex mix shifts: DC "box" capex ~$280B (2026) plateaus ~$580-590B (2028-30) while GPU/ASIC capex climbs $340B (2026) → >$800B (2030); >$3T of accelerator financings over 5 yrs (JPM "AI Capex 2.0", 2026-06-16). Base-case DC capacity now **138 GW through 2030** (up from 122 GW Nov), power still the binding constraint; ASICs ~42% of AI chip units 2026 → ~53% 2027 (ASIC units +109% y/y vs GPU +39%) (JPM, 2026-06-16). Corroborated by Redburn: AI-11 capex +69% (2026) → $869bn (2027, +23%), with YTD upward revisions of +39%/+55% to the 2026/2027 numbers (Redburn, 2026-06-17).
- **800V power-architecture spend layers on top.** The grid-to-rack 800V transition itself adds a multi-billion content layer: JPM sizes DC power-management TAM ~$20B (2025) → ~$45B (2028) at ~+30% CAGR, and notes DC IT load on track to **triple to ~300 GW by 2030** (~+25% CAGR, >35 GW/yr new capacity, Omdia) (JPM "800V", 2026-06-09). Citi models ~19% CAGR in AI DC deployments ~9 GW → ~21 GW (2025-30), DC analog/power-semi TAM ~$9B → ~$35B (30% CAGR) (Citi "800V power", 2026-06-15). See [800V DC Data-Center Power](800v-dc-power.md) for the architecture detail.
- **The grid — not generation — is the bottleneck.** Bernstein: "generation we don't see as the constraint, we see the grid as more of the bottleneck," with **>2,000 GW of projects in the FERC interconnection queue** and a **4–5 year wait** to connect anywhere in the US (Bernstein power names, 2026-06-18). Jefferies' real-world read is sharper: frustration with utility interconnection has "reached a boil," pushing customers to behind-the-meter generation, batteries, and "bring-your-own-generation" (Jefferies, 2026-05-08).
- **Power demand inflecting after two flat decades.** Bernstein models **+3% US power-demand CAGR to 2030** (range heard 2.5–4.5%), with data-center electricity roughly doubling from **~250 TWh to ~500 TWh** by 2030 on ~40 GW of incremental DC capacity (Bernstein power names, 2026-06-18). VST sees ERCOT load +5–6% and PJM +2–3% to 2030 (VST Q1 FY26, 2026-05-07).
- **Gas is the bridge; nuclear/geothermal the only long-run baseload displacement.** Survey of 50+ DC operators: **98% prefer grid (or grid + on-site backup)**, with reliability/uptime and speed-to-power the deciding factors — and "three years out is effectively unacceptable" (Bernstein, 2026-06-18). Behind-the-meter stays a **5–10% niche** (more expensive than grid), not the 50% some bulls model (Bernstein, 2026-06-18).
- **Nuclear is the scarce premium product.** CEG runs the largest US nuclear fleet (~22 GW, 96.8% capacity factor) with ~900–1,000 MW of uprates identified and the Crane (ex-Three Mile Island) restart progressing (CEG Q1 FY26, 2026-05-11). VST has **~3.8 GW of nuclear under 20-yr PPAs** (Amazon at Comanche Peak; Meta across Perry/Davis-Besse/Beaver Valley) (VST Q1 FY26). TLN's anchor is the **~1.9 GW front-of-the-meter AWS PPA at Susquehanna** (TLN Q1 FY26, 2026-05-05).
- **Gas-turbine OEMs sold out years forward with pricing power.** GEV ended Q1 with **100 GW of gas contracts** (44 GW firm + 56 GW SRAs), order book filled through 2027 with 2030 slots filling ahead of 2029 (permitting-gated); H1'26 orders priced **10–20 pts higher $/kW** y/y, plus a **~20–30% US price premium vs. Siemens** (GEV Q1 FY26, 2026-04-22; GEVernova preview, 2026-04-17). Industry-wide turbine capacity ramps 55 GW (2025) → 87 GW (2030), yet the US is still "~10 GW of gas short every year" (GEVernova preview, 2026-04-17).
- **Labor is now the #1 constraint.** Both Jefferies desks: labor — not turbines — is the gating factor; projects booked "three years in the future"; construction EPC names (Quanta, MasTec) seeing "shocking positive revisions," with Quanta's near-term growth talked up toward ~30% (Jefferies, 2026-05-08).
- **Satellite build-tracking: ~14–16 GW (2026), ~20 GW (2027) — far below the announced pipeline.** SinMax/Industrial Info's "Vulcan" platform (weekly satellite imagery + 500-analyst phone verification) tracks **11 GW built in 2025 → ~14–16 GW in 2026 → ~20 GW in 2027** (vs. ~40–45 GW *proposed* for 2027 — a **~50% haircut**, ~the same ~60% realization rate seen in renewables). The gate is **labor** (1–10 electricians/MW; ~1M total US electricians, 5 retiring per 1 entrant) plus permitting/policy (Maine/Michigan/Minnesota moratoria; only ~29% public support). Crucially **the misses are delays, not cancellations** (no peak signal); once construction starts, 2025 builds ran **~5 months faster than history**; ~74% are new (greenfield) sites that compound into later years. Behind-the-meter tracked at **~54 GW total**, 248 plants; natural-gas BTM ~25 GW starting construction in 2026 (~8 GW online '26, ~11 GW '27); turbine lead times 3–5 yrs. Top regions: Texas (~$1T proposed), Virginia, Ohio, Georgia, Utah; regulated markets now *faster* than deregulated (single approver vs. long queue) (Jefferies "DC build / Vulcan-IIR", 2026-06-22).
- **The behind-the-meter power-architecture expert view (Aman Chakri / ex-GE Vernova).** Gas "will be king" five years out; BTM is a **permanent** solution (not just a bridge) because connecting hyperscalers to the grid raises rates on the public and ISOs "pick their battles." Cost is ~the same BTM vs front-of-meter — **the variance is *time*, not cost** (interconnection takes 4–6 yrs). Installed all-in cost **~$2,900–3,200/kW (BTM), ~$3,000–4,000/kW** for aero/recip; technology cost alone >$2,000/kW (aero ~$2,200/kW). AI loads need **~10-millisecond response** that **no turbine/engine can meet** — so every architecture pairs gas with **UPS + battery storage** as a buffer (the developer claim of "no batteries needed" is a "flawed argument"). DCs spec **simple-cycle (not combined-cycle)** for fast start; **N+5 / ~40% overbuild** (install ~140 MW for 100 MW of demand) for 99.999% uptime; one large frame turbine beats 10 small aeros on $/kW except at high elevation (Wells Fargo "Aman Chakri BTM expert", 2026-05-27).
- **DC broker on the ground (Chris, DC broker/advisor).** ~**16 GW** of tracked demand, of which **~40% is firm for 2026–28** (rest 2029–31); **>50% of new AI-DC development goes international past 2028** (US lacks power/capacity); SE Asia/Canada/South America the beneficiaries. **~30% of his pipeline is true grid-islanded BTM**, never intending to connect. Build cost **~$9–10M/MW for CPU/cloud, ~$12–13M/MW for AI** DCs. The "big four" (AMZN/MSFT/GOOG/META) "get whatever they want"; **Oracle is the IG-credit swing** (3 colos dropped Oracle sales coverage); neoclouds need backstops/creative financing. Power-study feasibility cost went **$500 → $300,000** as utilities vet "tire-kickers" (185 GW in queue). xAI Colossus 122-day build is "not repeatable" (Chris was on the JPM DC call, 2026-05-04).
- **Gas-turbine vs. genset economics (Norbert Neuster, ex-Cummins Power Systems).** Gas is the only non-grid option that works for 26–28 builds (SMR/geothermal "not ready"). Diesel gensets are remarkably cheap/commoditized at **~$550–600/kW today (from ~$400–450 historically), ~$1,000+/kW fully enclosed** — and prices likely **fall back** as supply catches up (peak imbalance was 24–25; diesel lead times ~18 mos heading to 12–15). **CAT is shifting diesel build slots to gas** (gas accrues aftermarket hours/revenue), which could *extend* CAT diesel lead times. Generac (w/ Weichai engines) entering hard but channel/deployment capability — not product — gates share (cf. Kohler at only ~6–8% after years). On Bloom fuel cells: skeptical without a 30–50% cost cut from ~$2,700/kW (~3x gas); much of Bloom's 12-mo demand was "desperation." Batteries don't replace gensets but let you cut ~10–20% of them (Wells Fargo "Norbert Neuster turbine/genset expert", 2026-04-10).
- **Barclays: the capex peak gets pushed *beyond* 2028 — ~36 GW of next-gen compute, demand outstripping supply (Barclays "AI's S-Curve Is Steepening", 2026-06-01).** Barclays' AI-lab-demand-driven framework now has the **capex peak pushed farther out beyond 2028 with ~36 GW of next-gen compute infrastructure**, leveling thereafter — the near-term revenue ramp is driving inference demand and **putting stress on the capacity picture (demand outstripping supply)**, expected to balance more evenly next year. Their AI-XPU-specific capex grows **+90%/+65%/+21% CY26/27/28** (vs Street total hyperscaler capex +85%/+24%/+8%), GW added 8.8 (2025) → 16.6 (2026) → 27.5 (2027) → 33.3 (2028); Barclays hyperscaler capex >$1.1T in 2028 (~25%+ above Street). The most common question they hear is no longer ROI but **"what is your ability to upside supply to meet demand"** (Barclays "AI's S-Curve Is Steepening", 2026-06-01).
- **BofA: AI-DC-systems TAM ~$1.7Tn by CY30, +45% CAGR (BofA "AI 2030: Stronger for Longer", 2026-05-13).** BofA lifts CY30 **AI-DC-systems TAM to ~$1.7Tn from ~$1.4Tn prior** (+45% CAGR off $264bn in CY25), with AI servers ~75% ($1.3Tn), networking ~20% ($316bn) and storage ~5% ($81bn); accelerators ~$1.2Tn, **standalone server-CPU racks ~$110bn** (AI CPUs $88bn) and **SRAM-based ultra-low-latency racks coexisting with HBM-GPU racks** as workload tails elongate — an additive, more-diversified compute build, with **power/packaging/capital the named constraints** (BofA "AI 2030: Stronger for Longer", 2026-05-13).
- **Hyperscaler capex-per-gigawatt benchmark (JPM hyperscaler recap).** JPM frames DC build economics at **~$35B of capex per gigawatt** and **~$12B of cloud revenue per gigawatt per year**, with Google needing **~2.5–3 GW/yr of incremental capacity** to service its 1Q26 cloud-backlog surge (driving 27 capex to ~$290–295B from ~$200B in 26). Amazon flagged **memory/component cost inflation likely adding ~20%+ per-unit to 2027 capex** (same units, higher cost) — a read-through to rising $/MW build costs across the cohort (JPM hyperscaler recap, 2026-04-30).
- **SemiAnalysis: BTM power build accelerating to 40+ GW by 2028 as grid constraints force hyperscalers off-grid (SemiAnalysis, 2026-06-24).** SemiAnalysis published analysis on power grid constraints in the AI buildout, arguing that utility grid interconnection is so constrained that the **behind-the-meter (BTM) generation pipeline is on track to exceed 40 GW by 2028**. The analysis frames BTM not as a premium-for-speed solution but increasingly as the *default* path for large AI campus builds in constrained markets. Key structural read: if BTM approaches 40 GW (vs the Jefferies/SinMax satellite-tracked ~54 GW total BTM at various stages), it implies a step-change in gas-turbine demand (GEV, Caterpillar, Cummins) and generator/UPS content (Eaton, Vertiv) that is durable through at least 2030. The analysis dovetails with Jefferies' "bring-your-own-generation" framing (2026-05-08) and the Aman Chakri BTM architecture expert (interconnection 4–6 yrs → BTM is permanent, not a bridge). For the nuclear/power names: the BTM wave is not an alternative to nuclear — it is gas-turbine-led and validates CEG/VST's view that "we don't see generation as the constraint, we see the grid as more of the bottleneck" (Bernstein, 2026-06-18). (SemiAnalysis / Dylan Patel, 2026-06-24)

## Key debates

- **Bull / where the money is:** Demand is real, supply is static, and the scarce assets re-rate. CEG: ~$0.75T of data-center capital projected — "twice the size of the 3 largest listed US utilities" — and CEG sells "the product no one else has ready: firm, clean, 24/7 power, available now" (CEG Q3 FY25 / Q1 FY26). GEV is "an earnings-revisions machine," up off pace of EBITDA growth, with Electrification flipping from "peaking" to accelerating — TAM doubled $150B→$300B in a month and the $200B total-backlog target pulled forward to '27 (GEVernova callback, 2026-04-22). WMB: pipelines lag gas-gen, and "Williams is the ultimate manifestation of this thesis," compounding via 5x-build-multiple behind-the-meter gas for data centers (JSD via WMB page, 2026-05-06). PWR is the "pick-and-shovel" labor layer targeting >2x earnings power by 2030 (15–20% EPS CAGR) (PWR Q4 FY25, 2026-02-19).
- **Bear / what could break it:** Timing and slippage. Data-center delays are real — EIA, ERCOT and PJM all cut 2026 demand forecasts; "~50% of data centers delayed / canceled" headlines, though Jefferies calls this "normal course" (Jefferies, 2026-05-08). The big hyperscaler nuclear deals keep slipping (CEG promised announcements that haven't landed; some conversations "paused" on PJM regulatory clarity) (CEG Q1 FY26). Management teams are more cautious than the tape: VST says meaningful DC tightening doesn't begin until "late 2027 or early 2028" and load growth is "not at the extremely elevated levels… forecast by many third parties" (VST Q4 FY25). GEV order-peak risk is live — Q4'25's 24 GW / $22.2B order quarter is the comp to beat (GEVernova callback). Bernstein/Cantor procurement expert thinks the build pace is "plateauing" — "I think last year was the peak," with power and people the two gates (Bernstein DC buildout, 2026-06-11). And policy/rate-payer politics bite in an election year: PJM bills rose ~$20/mo, IPPs that signed rate-payer-protection pledges can't contract existing resources, and IPPs "are not participating in the rally" (Jefferies, 2026-05-08).
- **Timeline / inflection points:** Behind-the-meter and gas-gen scramble *now* (band-aid, 5–10% of market); large H-class turbines stay under-supplied through 2030 (relative positive), while diesel gensets and small on-site gen move to over-supply by 2027–2029 (Bernstein power names, 2026-06-18). Nuclear restarts/uprates land 2027–2034. Grid/electrification is the multi-decade compounder beyond 2030 (Bernstein, 2026-06-18). The cleanest near-term tell on supply: track the chip ramp and the order/backlog cadence at GEV (Q2 FY26 print, ~late July).

**Read-through — orbital data centers (not a near-term escape valve):** with terrestrial DCs power/water/permitting-constrained, "space compute" has moved from sci-fi into US-equity discussion (cheaper launch via Starship/reusables). FundaAI's expert work argues the binding constraint is *not* launch cost but **heat rejection**: in vacuum there's no convection, so cooling is radiation-only — a 1 GW orbital DC implies ~0.5–1.5 km² of radiator + ~2.9 km² of solar + ~2,000–3,000 kg/s coolant flow, dozens of launches, in-orbit assembly, and finite (~7–10 yr) asset life. Verdict: a long-dated *option* (1 MW demo plausible soon; GW-class far off), to be de-risked by engineering milestones, not modeled as near-term capacity. Only real beneficiary is SpaceX (private; vertical integration) — no listed read-through (FundaAI "Deep|Space: Orbital Data Centers", 2026-06-22).
