# Theme — Custom ASIC / TPU (vs merchant GPU)

_Wiki · generated 2026-06-19 · cross-company theme · sources: equity calls (`../_equity_calls/Semis`), briefing roll-ups (`../_briefings/by-ticker`), earnings transcripts, company pages. Company pages: [../00_INDEX.md](../00_INDEX.md)._

## What it is / why it matters
Every frontier-model builder and hyperscaler is now running a two-track silicon strategy: buy merchant GPUs (NVDA) for flexibility and time-to-market, and co-design custom accelerators (ASIC/XPU) for their highest-volume, most-predictable workloads to claw back margin, power efficiency and supply control. The buy-side reason to care is that this is the single biggest swing factor in how the ~$3–4T/yr end-of-decade AI infra TAM (Jensen's framing, NVDA Q1 FY27, 2026-05-20) gets split — and it pits a high-margin merchant monopoly (NVDA ~75% GM) against a lower-margin but stickier custom-silicon complex (AVGO, MRVL, Google's internal COT team). The fight is not winner-take-all: the consensus shape is a durable split (Arcuri/UBS pegs ~70/30 GPU/ASIC long-term, 2026-06-04), but the *marginal* gigawatt increasingly skews custom, and that marginal mix is what moves the stocks.

Sherman's MediaTek-vs-Qualcomm/Broadcom share math (by compute-die count, not chips): **6% (2026) → 16/17% (2027) → 30% (2028)**, with the **swing factor being 400G SerDes** — Broadcom is still the only 400G supplier, so if Google adopts 400G in the **V9** generation it would cap MediaTek's gains, but Sherman's checks suggest Google only adopts 400G in **V10** by late, leaving room for MediaTek share through V9 (Redburn, 2026-06-23). TrendForce corroborates the procurement shift: Google historically bought only conventional DRAM and relied entirely on **Broadcom for HBM**, but **MediaTek (TPU V8/V9) is now increasingly handling HBM procurement directly**, with MediaTek "aggressively discussing HBM3E/HBM4E with Samsung" (StoneX/TrendForce "Memory expert call — Avril", 2026-06-23).
- **Google did *not* manage to buy HBM direct — stays via Broadcom + MediaTek for ~3 years (Avril, 2026-06-23).** TrendForce: Google **attempted direct HBM procurement but found it too challenging**, so for the next ~3 years HBM procurement remains via Broadcom and MediaTek (the "~20% of Google" figure includes both, all flowing to Google) (StoneX/TrendForce "Memory expert call — Avril", 2026-06-23). The open buy-side question Avril took offline (to follow up in 48h): whether Google going direct would strip **Broadcom's HBM markup** — Broadcom delivers a full TPU package and can put a markup on the HBM price the way NVIDIA marks up its own; if Google ever went direct, Broadcom would forego that (StoneX/TrendForce, 2026-06-23). She does *not* see Google wanting to go direct, so the markup risk is latent, not imminent.
- **Anthropic's Micron/SK Hynix/Samsung memory deals imply an in-house ASIC (Avril, 2026-06-23).** TrendForce reads the **Anthropic–Micron deal — server DRAM + enterprise SSD (2027) plus HBM (2028)** — as direct evidence that **Anthropic is building its own in-house ASIC** (the HBM line only makes sense if Anthropic has its own accelerator), and Anthropic is striking the **same combined deal with SK Hynix and Samsung** (StoneX/TrendForce "Memory expert call — Avril", 2026-06-23). This is incremental to the existing read that Anthropic's near-term silicon runs through Broadcom Sunfish + AWS Trainium — a possible third, self-designed leg further out.

SOCAMM, LPDDR5, NAND). The risk: hyperscalers push to **consign HBM directly** to compress NVIDIA's revenue/EPS (the same logic that drives Google to MediaTek), so "the stock that looks cheap isn't so cheap anymore" — and SemiAnalysis's report on NVIDIA potentially cutting the SOCAMM spec fits this BOM-pressure narrative (Redburn "Conor O'Mara Asia Trip Takeaways", 2026-06-23).

## Key debates
- **Bull / where the money is:** Custom silicon wins the *marginal* gigawatt because it gives frontier labs better compute-per-dollar and per-watt on their highest-volume workloads, and labs will cut discretionary off-the-shelf GPU buys before their strategic ASIC programs when capex tightens (Sur, 2026-06-17). AVGO is the cleanest expression — guaranteed rising Google revenue through 2031, V8i ramping now, V9 2nm into C28, plus a near-monopoly (~70–75%) on the attached scale-up/scale-out/scale-across networking that rides on *every* TPU rack regardless of who designs the compute die (Sur, 2026-06-05; 2026-06-04). AMZN's Trainium adds a second, price/perf-led leg with optionality on external rack sales.
- **Bear / what could break it:** Arcuri (UBS) would *buy NVDA over AVGO* if forced to choose (2026-06-04): the Google-diversification drumbeat "only gets louder," AVGO's business is lumpier/more concentrated (Google + a few labs), and AVGO's multiple "still has more to come down" while NVDA's has already collapsed. NVDA's degrees of freedom are real — it can drop a $20B Vera CPU program "out of thin air" and get supply, while AVGO shows back-end/substrate/packaging constraints (Arcuri). Share-in-a-growing-market is a weak bear: Arcuri argues share only matters in a mature market, and NVDA is *gaining* share at Anthropic, the recognized frontier leader.
- **The Google COT / MediaTek wildcard.** Google's internal **CLT/COT team** (using MediaTek for ~10% of the design) builds the *training* track — the V8T "Zerafish" (1 compute die / 6 HBM, ~2x less complex than Broadcom's V8i). Sur's checks say it still needs further optimization and likely *another respin* of both compute and I/O die, and that Google's intent is supplier *optionality* (6–8 ASIC programs running at any time), not replacing Broadcom on the highest-complexity inferencing roadmap (2026-06-17). Bear read (briefings): longer-term COT-model bypass risk is the structural overhang on AVGO ([../AVGO.md](../AVGO.md)). Note the inversion vs. intuition: inferencing is now the *more* complex/higher-ASP track (agentic, more SRAM, embedded ARM cores), which is the track Broadcom keeps (Sur, 2026-06-05). Dylan Patel (SemiAnalysis, ~2026-06) reframes the wildcard as *deeper than dual-sourcing*: Google runs **three genuinely distinct TPU architectures** — AVGO's, MediaTek's, and a **third, "very different" undisclosed architecture** — a deliberate hedge against any one design hitting a co-design "local minimum," which both raises the structural COT/optionality risk to AVGO *and* argues no single competitor sweeps the roadmap.
- **Why Hock won't raise FY27.** Despite better visibility, AVGO did *not* raise its ">$100B" FY27 AI guide — the omission, not the print, drove the **-12.6%** post-Q2 reaction. Sur reads this as (a) Hock's style (won't ratchet every quarter, a dig at MRVL) and (b) multi-GW greenfield programs now gated by *power/data-center readiness*, not chip supply (2026-06-17; Arcuri concurs, 2026-06-04).
- **Timeline / inflection:** V8i Sunfish volume now → V9 2nm ramps C28 → OpenAI ASIC tapes out late-2026, ships 2H27, big ramp 2028 (Arcuri, 2026-06-04). The ASIC-vs-GPU mix debate "is not going to go away — it gets louder and louder" (Arcuri, 2026-06-04).
- **NVDA has *not* lost share despite the ASIC drumbeat — and could weaponize open-source against ASICs.** Clark Tang's Computex/GTC takeaway: the framing flipped from "NVDA vs ASIC, one wins" to coexistence — everyone assumed NVDA loses share dramatically (revenue/GW/unit), but over the last few years it has **held share handsomely, and adjusting for Anthropic (which barely uses NVDA) it likely *gained* share in 2025–26**. Gavin Baker's twist: NVDA "any day it wanted" could ship a frontier open-source model — if labs' own ASIC economics depend on capturing model margin, a strong NVDA open-source model "joins the frontier" and erodes the revenue/margin that funds custom ASIC programs ("that's a cute ASIC you've built there") (BG2 podcast, 2026-06).
- **"Are NVIDIA and OpenAI losers to Google or kingmakers?"** Bernstein frames the merchant-TPU shift as a genuine threat to the incumbent stack: the one-time hyperscale *laggard* now "appears the leader in AI cloud" with **Google's vertically integrated (TPU) solution neck-and-neck with Microsoft for incremental cloud dollars added Q/Q** (GCP added ~$2.4B Q/Q vs Azure ~$2.5B, AWS ~$2.0B; GCP grew 63% Y/Y). MSFT's own custom silicon (Maia) remains the laggard of the three vs GOOG/AMZN — Bernstein's silicon highlights this quarter are Google TPU and AWS Trainium, with no Maia traction called out (Bernstein "Cloud in the Quarter", 2026-06-22).
- **OpenAI launches "Jalapeño" — the first frontier-lab-owned inference ASIC, built with Broadcom (FinTwit / @gdb, 2026-06-24).** OpenAI announced its first custom AI chip, codenamed **"Jalapeño,"** designed from scratch in **9 months** for LLM inference (design process itself accelerated by OpenAI's own models) and manufactured in partnership with **Broadcom (AVGO)**. This is the first time a frontier lab has publicly revealed a first-generation ASIC it owns the IP for. Key structural implications: (1) **AVGO confirms OpenAI XPU program** — previously disclosed as taping out late-2026; the branding/announcement confirms the design is sufficiently mature; the deployment timeline aligns with Hock's FY28-step-up framing and Sur's "OpenAI compute ≈ Anthropic's" call; (2) **MSFT inherits Jalapeño IP through 2031** via its OpenAI equity deal terms — positioning Microsoft as an indirect beneficiary of AVGO's custom silicon without being a direct ASIC customer; (3) **CBRS (Cerebras) bear signal**: OpenAI's core inference niche was the $25B take-or-pay anchor; if OpenAI gradually routes fast-inference load onto Jalapeño, Cerebras's call that "inference demand underwrites all the capacity" is weakened. (4) The 9-month design timeline (vs industry norms of 18-24 months) appears to be enabled by AI-accelerated EDA — a signal that the *barrier-to-entry* for custom ASIC is falling. (@OpenAI; @gdb; @PatrickMoorhead, 2026-06-24)
- **MediaTek wins Google TPU v9 primary orders on SerDes execution (FinTwit / @dnystedt, 2026-06-24).** A concrete supply-chain datapoint: MediaTek won the primary production orders for Google's **TPU v9** by delivering a custom **336G SerDes** solution after Broadcom's more ambitious **448G SerDes design missed the production deadline**. This is a distinct bear datapoint for AVGO's near-term share at Google beyond the HBM-bundle-cost mechanism (Conor/Redburn, 2026-06-23): it adds an *execution risk* dimension — Broadcom targeted a more aggressive spec and lost the timeline race. A Google employee interview cited by @RihardJarc argued that the real value of ASIC designers like AVGO is now their **TSMC CoWoS allocation, not the co-design premium** — a structurally bearish reframe for AVGO's "design expertise = sustained ASP premium" thesis. Watch whether V10 (all-N2, 400G SerDes) is another high-bar spec that creates a fresh opening for MediaTek. (@dnystedt; @RihardJarc, 2026-06-24)
- **QCOM enters datacenter connectivity with C1000 CPU (QCOM Investor Day, 2026-06-24).** Qualcomm revealed at its investor day a **datacenter CPU** based on its Oryon architecture, targeting hyperscalers and large enterprises. The **C1000 (Oryon CPU for DC)** is targeting **2028 delivery**, with a stated **$15B DC revenue target in FY2029**. Key facts: META signed an MOU as the first big-tech DC customer; Satya Nadella endorsed QCOM HBC XPU deployment in Azure. The datacenter connectivity angle: QCOM is entering the scale-out fabric adjacent to its HBC XPU — a potential third-party SerDes/networking supplier in a space currently dominated by Broadcom and Marvell. From the ASIC theme's perspective: a new competitor for the connectivity attach (not for ASIC design/fab), but one that arrives in 2028 and starts from zero market share; the more meaningful read is QCOM validating that DC silicon is attractive enough to disrupt its own CPE/mobile economics. (@PatrickMoorhead; QCOM Investor Day, 2026-06-24)
- **Broadcom is the #2 HBM bit-demand driver after Nvidia — the accelerator-side ASIC/HBM linkage, quantified (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall", 2025-08-11).** SemiAnalysis names **Broadcom as the #2 driver of HBM bit demand after Nvidia**, via its TPU (Google) and MTIA (Meta) design-service volumes (p.4) — the earliest on-disk primary-source confirmation of the AVGO-HBM-demand linkage this page otherwise builds out through the 2026 JPM/Sur/Fubon/Jefferies cadence. **Broadcom and Marvell are named as the go-to SerDes IP sources for hyperscalers** building custom silicon (p.20) — the IP-licensing layer underneath both companies' design-service businesses. On base-die differentiation: **Marvell is flagged as the first company to reveal a custom HBM base-die solution**, using a **die-to-die (D2D) PHY built on 1/4 the standard shoreline** (i.e. a denser, non-standard interconnect footprint vs the reference HBM base-die design) (p.27) — a concrete technical marker for MRVL's HBM base-die differentiation strategy that predates and complements the CXL/Trainium/Maia roadmap datapoints already on this page.
- **Amazon's HBM procurement strategy: buy direct, trade performance for cost (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall", 2025-08-11).** Amazon's HBM-procurement approach is **direct procurement from the memory makers rather than routing through a design partner** (the AVGO/Broadcom-style consignment model Google and others use) — a deliberate cost-lowering choice (p.4, 46). The report flags **Trainium2e as the one socket where Samsung retains the majority of HBM3E share**, framing it as evidence that **Amazon is willing to trade some performance for cost** by leaning on Samsung's HBM3E even where it may trail SK Hynix on spec (p.4, 46). This is a useful mechanism-level explanation for AMZN's "price/perf" positioning already tracked elsewhere on this page (Bernstein's 30-40% better price/perf framing) — the HBM-sourcing choice is one lever behind that cost advantage, not just the compute-die design.
- **Google TPU's design philosophy: conservative and reliability-first, not redline like merchant GPUs (SemiAnalysis "Scaling the Memory Wall", 2025-08-11).** SemiAnalysis characterizes Google's TPU design philosophy as **conservative and reliability-first**, explicitly contrasted with merchant GPUs which are pushed closer to the operating "redline" for maximum performance (p.40-41). Useful context for reconciling TPU's efficiency-per-watt claims (tracked elsewhere on this page via JPM's ~2x perf/watt-vs-GPU figures) with a design culture that prioritizes uptime/yield over peak spec — consistent with Google's own internal "COT" optionality strategy (running three parallel TPU architectures, per Dylan Patel/SemiAnalysis) rather than betting everything on one aggressive design.
- **CPU-side custom silicon — the other half of the hyperscaler vertical-integration story (SemiAnalysis/Wong & Patel, "CPUs are Back: The Datacenter CPU Landscape in 2026", 2026-02-09).** Everything above is the accelerator/XPU side of custom silicon; hyperscalers are running the identical playbook one layer down, on the CPU. **AWS Graviton5**: **192 Neoverse V3 cores, 172B transistors, built on TSMC 3nm** — and Amazon is using Graviton as the **head-node CPU for Trainium3 racks at a 1:4 CPU:XPU ratio** (p.44-48), i.e. Graviton isn't just a general-purpose EC2 CPU anymore, it's becoming load-bearing infrastructure for the Trainium accelerator fleet this page already tracks. **Google Axion**: two variants — **C4A (up to 72-96 Neoverse V2 cores)** and **N4A (64 Neoverse N3 cores)** — with SemiAnalysis floating the forward claim that **Axion could become the head-node CPU for TPU clusters serving Gemini**, the direct CPU-side analog to Graviton/Trainium (p.50-51). **ARM Phoenix**: ARM's **own first full in-house chip design** — a **128-core Neoverse V3** part — with **Meta as first customer**, and ARM separately **designing a custom CPU for OpenAI** via the Stargate/SoftBank venture (p.56-57). Phoenix is the most structurally notable of the three: it extends "custom silicon" beyond accelerators into CPUs (already true of Graviton/Axion), but also extends the *customer base* beyond hyperscalers directly into AI labs (OpenAI) — the same frontier-lab-owned-silicon pattern this page tracks via OpenAI's Jalapeño XPU (Broadcom-built), now appearing on the CPU side too, with ARM itself as the design house rather than a hyperscaler's internal team. Read across: the CPU and XPU custom-silicon threads are converging — Graviton/Trainium and (prospectively) Axion/TPU are being co-designed as paired head-node/accelerator systems, and ARM's Phoenix-for-OpenAI shows the frontier labs extending their silicon ambitions to the CPU layer at the same time they stand up their first XPUs.
