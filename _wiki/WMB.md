## Snapshot
Williams is a US natural-gas midstream pure-play: interstate transmission, gathering & processing (G&P), storage, and gas marketing. Four reportable segments — **Transmission, Power & Gulf; Northeast G&P; West; and Gas & NGL Marketing Services** — plus "Other" (upstream, corporate) (10-K, 2026-02-24). The crown jewel is **Transco**, the largest US interstate gas pipeline by volume, running Gulf-to-Northeast and structurally short of capacity in the markets it serves. The franchise has pivoted hard into two demand-driven legs: (1) **gas transmission expansions** (Transco, Northwest Pipeline/Mountain West) feeding power-gen and LNG, and (2) a newer **Power Innovation (PI)** behind-the-meter business — building gas generation directly for hyperscaler data centers at a targeted ~5x EBITDA build multiple. Barclays frames WMB as "the poster child of the data-center-driven, gas-to-power story," with rising generalist investor interest (Barclays, T. Chen, 2026-01-21). Williams is positioned at the intersection of the AI/power capex cycle and the US LNG export ramp.

## Current state (Q1 FY2026, call 2026-05-05)
Record quarter. Adjusted EBITDA **$2.25bn, +13% YoY** (from $1.99bn); adjusted EPS **+22%** (John Porter, CFO, Q1 FY26). Transmission & Gulf led, +~$150m / ~17% — Transco +~10% YoY on the post-rate-case tariff step-up plus expansions; Deepwater Gulf +60%; storage +35%; West +16% (Haynesville / Louisiana Energy Gateway). Sequent marketing $227m EBITDA. Upstream Haynesville divested to JERA (closed Jan-26; ~$180m book gain excluded from recurring metrics).

**Guidance:** now pointing to the **upper half** of original FY26 adjusted EBITDA guide; reaffirmed the Analyst-Day **10%+ EBITDA & EPS CAGR (2025–2030)**. Management says the *contracted* base CAGR moved from ~8% (Feb Analyst Day) to **~9%** with the new Q1 projects, and called that "still pretty conservative" (Porter, Q1 FY26). Seasonally soft 2Q expected before H2 reacceleration, including the partial Socrates startup in Q3.

**Backlog / projects (Q1 FY26):** Three new majors plus an upsize. **NEO** — fifth PI project, 682MW, 12.5-yr contract, ~$2.3bn, ISD H2-2028, 5x build multiple (largest PI deal to date). **Atlas** — Transco gas-for-backup-power (displacing diesel) at a Northeast data center, up to 164 MMcf/d, 13-yr term, <$50m capex, in service by YE26. **Silver Spur** — Northwest Pipeline expansion into Idaho, 275 MMcf/d, ~$0.5bn, ISD early-2030 (phase 1 of Rockies Columbia Connector). **Power Express** (Transco/Virginia) upsized to 750 MMcf/d, online 2030. ~700 MMcf/d of G&P expansions sanctioned in the quarter. Total committed PI capital ~$5.1bn as of Q3-25 (Q3 FY25 call). Growth capex midpoint raised to **$7.3bn** for FY26.

**PI sizing / project detail (Barclays, T. Chen):** As of Jan-26, **$5.1bn of sanctioned BTM projects ≈ at least ~1.5 GW installed** (Barclays, 2026-01-21). Project-level economics: **Meta Prometheus** (New Albany, OH) — WMB investment upsized from $1.6bn to **$2.0bn** to supply ~400 MW on-site gas power plus supporting infrastructure (~516 MW nameplate / ~400 MW derated equipment), completion 2026; two further PI projects (Socrates $2.0bn ISD 4Q26; Aquila/Apollo $3.1bn ISD 1Q27) for an unnamed investment-grade counterparty, **~800 MW combined** on 10-yr, primarily fixed-price PPAs, completion ~1H27 (Barclays, 2025-10-31). Barclays pegs implied build economics ~$4mm/MW including supporting gas infra for Prometheus, and estimates the three projects add **>$1bn run-rate EBITDA** at the 5x multiple — **~13% growth vs the 2025 guide midpoint ($7.75bn)** (Barclays, 2026-01-21).

**Turbine supply lock-up (Barclays):** WMB holds a **5 GW turbine reservation with Siemens Energy** across various turbine types to feed US BTM data-center projects — the supply-chain edge underpinning the pipeline (Barclays, 2026-01-21). Barclays estimates the **~3.5 GW of *incremental* BTM capacity** beyond the sanctioned ~1.5 GW could add **at least ~$2.5bn of annual EBITDA over time**, with additional turbine capacity reportedly on hold with Caterpillar as further upside. WMB CEO Chad Zamarin sat on Siemens Energy's Nov-25 CMD AI-power panel (with Meta and Dominion), where panelists downplayed bubble/overcapacity concerns and stressed grid/pipeline constraints and an affordability-balanced all-of-the-above energy approach (Barclays, 2026-01-21).

**Income-investor framing (Barron's "Best Income Investments Now," Andrew Bary, 2026-06-29 issue):** Ted Gardner, senior portfolio manager at Westwood Holdings Group (runs the Westwood Salient MLP & Energy Infrastructure fund), "favors Williams, the country's leading natural-gas pipeline company. It aims to generate **10% annualized growth in Ebitda through 2030**, one of the better outlooks in the sector." Sector context in the same piece: pipeline revenue is largely on long-term contracts, insulating it from energy-price swings, though the sector overall could see "more muted returns in the second half of 2026" after large H1 gains driven partly by power demand for data centers and LNG exports (Barron's, Andrew Bary, 2026-06-29).

**Balance sheet:** Leverage **4.1x**, modestly above the 3.5–4.0x target — a 26/27 timing issue per management, with "historic earnings growth" in 28+ resetting capacity. Financing options under review (partnership/JV around PI, capital recycling); specifics promised "in the next couple of months" (Porter, Q1 FY26). Dividend growth explicitly stays intact.

## Debate / thesis
- **Bull (JSD equity call, 2026-05-06; corroborated on the Q1 call):** Pipelines are a *lagging* indicator vs. gas-gen, and "Williams is the ultimate manifestation of this thesis" — a long-cycle compounder where the big EBITDA is long-dated (Silver Spur/Power Express run-rate only in 2031–32). JSD argues the financially-levered structure means EPS CAGR should run "north of 20%" — well above the stated 10% — and that the Street floor moving 8%→9% is management breadcrumbing; his desk models ~13% EBITDA CAGR. PI is an affordability/"behind-the-meter" winner that *aligns* with the political cycle (vs. FTM/IPP losers). Optionality is fungible: if transmission stays hard to build, the BTM window widens; if FTM unlocks, the pipelines expand. Contract durations lengthening (12.5yr → 15–20yr discussions), build multiples ~5x with capex-per-kW now falling = more competitive + lower terminal recontracting risk. Pac-Northwest (Idaho/WA/OR) and a latent Northeast/New England pipeline need are unpriced upside. JSD survey: ~80/20 bullish; consensus sees Williams beating its 10% CAGR by 200–300bps and FID'ing ~$3bn more PI capex this year.
- **Bear (pushbacks captured on the JSD call):** Expensive — 13–14x EBITDA, "fundamental/energy guys" see good growth but limited upside; "generals" chasing torque rotate to smaller, higher-operating-leverage BTM/compression names instead. PI terminal-value risk: contracts are "only" 10–12.5yr and what happens at recontracting (vs. grid economics) is an open question — the predominant bear thesis is that PI is *lower-quality* earnings. Skepticism the 6GW backlog is being replenished (mgmt declined to give a precise post-NEO figure). Mid-term de-rating risk on 2026/2028 election cycles and a gas-on-vibes peak; gas-demand momentum decelerates in H2. Leverage at 4.1x (peaking ~4.3x in 27 per JSD desk) needs the back-end ramp to land.
- **Where the sell-side stands:** **Barclays (T. Chen): Equal Weight** (rating as of 2026-01-21; price referenced USD 61.51 on 2026-01-20) — constructive on the BTM growth optionality (5 GW Siemens reserve, >$1bn near-term run-rate EBITDA, ~$2.5bn incremental over time) but rated EW. No other broker PTs/ratings in the disk sources (briefing coverage is nil for WMB). Sell-side voices *present on the Q1 call*: JPMorgan (Tonet), Jefferies (Dumlin Smith), Wells Fargo, BMO, Scotiabank, Citi, Wolfe, BofA, Goldman (McKay), UBS, Seaport, Tuohy — uniformly engaged/constructive, debate centered on PI financing, backlog cadence and contract duration, not on whether the story works. The JSD desk is emphatically long, arguing Street numbers (now ~9% CAGR) are still too low.
- **Wells Fargo midstream desk (WFC "Friday wrap / midstream", 2026-06-05):** rates WMB **Overweight** and flags rising debate on the proposed PI-JV (added Cheniere + KGS to top picks; removed Targa). New attributed detail: WFC's BTM valuation work implies **~15% upside for both Williams and KGS if they hit their stated 2030 BTM power targets** — the equity values reflect *some* but not all of the upside; WFC estimates **Williams is discounting ~5 of its 8 GW** BTM target (Kodiak ~1.4–2.0 of ~2 GW). WFC uses **9.5x** for the WMB power-business sum-of-the-parts (8.5x for Kodiak), backing into multiples via DCF (10-yr contract / no terminal value ≈ 7x; 15 yrs ≈ 9x; 20 yrs ≈ 10x). Macro frame: ~**125 GW incremental US AI power demand by 2030**, ~30% BTM ≈ 37 GW, of which the aggregated BTM-provider targets (~18 GW) is the truly grid-islanded slice. The hosted DC expert said BTM is now **~40% of power deals**, and post-grid-connection BTM assets likely run at only **15–30% capacity for peak-shaving** under interruptible tariffs — directly relevant to WMB's PI terminal-value (recontracting) bear point. Turbine-backlog color: **more canceled/freed turbine reservation slots** (~30–35 projects nationwide canceled/delayed; Kodiak picked up freed slots on two occasions), with secondary-market capacity available at a premium (WFC "midstream Friday wrap", 2026-06-05). Investor feedback on the WMB PI-JV was **"lukewarm"** — questions on *why* they're doing it and the low upfront multiple; the read: fine if paired with a large announcement, risk of a sell-off if done in isolation.

- Next print: Q2 FY26 (early Aug-26, seasonal trough).

## Risks
- **PI terminal value / earnings quality** — recontracting economics after 10–12.5yr BTM terms vs. eventual grid access (the core bear point).
- **Leverage timing** — 4.1x now, peaking before the 28 ramp; financing must land without diluting or selling high-quality assets cheaply.
- **Turbine supply-chain / single-vendor** — the BTM pipeline leans on the 5 GW Siemens Energy reservation (plus CAT backup); turbine delivery slippage or cost inflation would directly hit ISD timing and the ~$4mm/MW build economics (Barclays, 2026-01-21).
- **Gas supply/demand & price** — dependence on basin supply and end-market demand; commodity volatility can pressure long-term transport/storage contracts and throughput (10-K risk summary, 2026-02-24).
- **Growth execution** — explicit 10-K risk on pursuing and operationally implementing PI projects; supply-chain/turbine and labor cost inflation (capex-per-kW rising before bottoming, per JSD).
- **Permitting/litigation** — interstate projects exposed to state 401 vetoes and prolonged litigation (mgmt cited 13 years on Atlantic Sunrise).
- **Counterparty/credit** and **competition** (10-K risk summary). **Policy/election** risk to the gas-build thesis (JSD pushback).
